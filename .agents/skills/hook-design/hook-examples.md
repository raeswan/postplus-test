## 1. boldClaim

**Skill mapping:** `hook-design` → `boldClaim` mechanism

### Overview

A bold statement hook opens with a declarative sentence that challenges conventional wisdom or makes a surprising claim.

It creates cognitive dissonance: the viewer registers the statement, compares it to existing beliefs, and pauses to evaluate whether the claim is credible. That pause is the 3-second hold.

**When to use:**
- Addressing common mistakes, debunking myths, sharing contrarian advice
- Educational content, industry commentary, how-to videos where authority matters
- Not suitable for pure entertainment or storytelling (authoritative tone feels out of place)

**Requirements:**
- The claim must be backed up within the first 10-15 seconds
- Specific enough to spark curiosity, broad enough to reach a meaningful audience
- Too vague: "You're doing TikTok wrong" → ineffective
- Specific enough: "Most creators are killing their reach with captions" → triggers curiosity

**Common failure modes:**
- Claim has no proof path to follow up
- Tone too authoritative for entertainment content
- Vague provocation with no concrete target

### Reference Examples

**Best / #1 claims:**
- The internet's #1 [product type].
- The best way to [accomplish the goal of the product].
- What makes [the product type] the best?
- My skin has never looked better with [product].
- The best way to find X in 2022.
- [Product] changed how I do [task], and I'm never going back.
- Why is [product] so good though?
- After hours of researching, I found the best [product type] for [task].
- I found the best [product category] for [value prop].
- Must-have [products] for [X].
- The best [target audience] know something that you don't.

**Strong declarative claims:**
- This is going to blow your mind.
- 99% of your [target audience] don't. To be the 1% you need to [X].
- Why 99% of [target audience] won't [X].
- Here is the exact [X] you need as an [X].
- This [product] is the secret to [X].
- I will show you how to have the best [X] of your life.
- I'm about to give you the biggest cheat sheet on...
- Here's what I would do if I was [action].
- Rule #1 of being a [X] is never to [X].
- I'm going to teach you how to...
- The ultimate [X] checklist to [action].
- Reasons why you should [X].
- My secret to [popular trend] revealed!
- Here are the 3 best ways to [X].
- Simple [X] that will make you [X].
- Everybody is looking for [fundamental human desire].

**Comparative claims:**
- Your X isn't [adverb]; you just need X.
- Instead of focusing on [X], focus on [X].
- Here's the difference between [X] and [X].
- I kept experiencing [pain point], so I tried this instead!
- If you have [pain point] — you need to see this!
- Something that's always annoyed me about X.

**Common mistake alerts:**
- The biggest mistake I made was when I...
- If I had known this sooner...
- Here are [X] mistakes you might be making...
- The worst mistake you can make in [subject]
- Avoid these common pitfalls in [topic]

## 2. questionGap

**Skill mapping:** `hook-design` → `questionGap` mechanism

### Overview

A question hook opens by asking something the target audience urgently wants answered. It leverages the curiosity gap by creating an information void that the viewer's brain wants to fill.

**Requirements:**
- Must be specific, relevant, and impossible to answer with yes/no
- "Want more views?" → too generic, obvious answer, no tension
- "Why do some TikToks go viral with 200 views while others die at 20,000?" → specific, implies there's a knowable reason
- The best question hooks hint at the answer's value without giving it away
- Avoid clickbait feel — viewers have strong filters for manipulative hooks

**When to use:**
- Audience has a clear but unresolved question
- The answer can be delivered within a short time
- Opening an information gap rather than making a dramatic claim

**Common failure modes:**
- Generic questions with obvious answers
- Sounds like clickbait, lacks specificity

### Reference Examples

**Curiosity gap:**
- Ever wondered how [action] is done?
- Have you ever heard the story behind [topic]?
- What would you do if...
- Is there anything worse than [X]?
- Does anyone else hate [X]?
- Am I the only one who...
- Have you ever had to [X]?
- I didn't know X could be related to X.
- What's inside [product]?
- I just found out [fact about product category].
- Common questions I get about [product].

**Decision / choice:**
- Thinking of trying [product type]?
- Are you [accomplishing the goal optimally]?
- How to find time for X.
- How to get X done in just 10 minutes.
- Struggling to do [task]?
- Why is it challenging and costly to find a good [product category]?
- Searching for an affordable [product type]? Check this out!
- How to locate an affordable [service].

**Fact / awareness:**
- Did you know? [fact about product category].
- Are you one of [fact about product category] people who do X?
- Why is it important to [do product-related task]?
- Wait, have you tried X?
- [Task] has never been easier than with [product].
- How to easily [task].

## 3. proofFirst

**Skill mapping:** `hook-design` → `proofFirst` mechanism

### Overview

A proof-first hook leads with evidence, results, or credentials before explaining what the video is about. It establishes authority and credibility immediately, making viewers more willing to invest attention.

**Structure:** Show the result in the first 2 seconds, then tease how it was achieved.

**Requirements:**
- The more specific and timely the proof, the better
- Weak: "I've helped hundreds of creators"
- Strong: "I helped three creators hit 100,000 views last week using this hook formula"
- Visual evidence strengthens credibility (screenshots, analytics, before/after)
- Don't make claims that sound too good to be true — unless you can back them up immediately
- If the claim feels exaggerated, viewers assume dishonesty and trust is lost

**When to use:**
- Trust is the main barrier
- Visible evidence or numbers can appear early
- The result itself is more compelling than the process

**Common failure modes:**
- Exaggerated proof with no credibility
- Fake before/after

### Reference Examples

**Data / results:**
- I tested every [product category] so you don't have to: here's what I found.
- I tested the viral [product type] to see if it lives up to the hype.
- This [product type] has over 5,000 reviews… let's see if it's worth it.
- How I got [X] in 24 hours.
- I tried this new method and...
- Here's how I achieved [X] in [timeframe].
- [Publication] can't stop raving about us.
- It's so good it sold out in a week.
- New customers get [discount].
- Take [discount] off when you try [product].

**Social proof:**
- TikTok made me try [product].
- Things TikTok made me try #13.
- This [product type] is going viral on [social media platform].
- This is your sign to [X].
- I literally cannot go a day without [X].
- This [X] is a game changer.

**Announcements:**
- PSA: [statement about product category].
- I'm revealing my top secrets to [topic].
- Here's my biggest life hack for X.
- Allow me to introduce you to [product].
- Now you can get X delivered right to your door.
- Guys, it's here…
- The secret to [X] revealed.

## 4. painRecognition

**Skill mapping:** `hook-design` → `painRecognition` mechanism

### Overview

A pain recognition hook directly names the pain or frustration the audience is living with. Its power comes from **self-recognition**: when viewers hear a sentence that precisely describes their situation, they stop to confirm "that's me." This mechanism does not need novelty — it needs specificity.

**Requirements:**
- Be specific: add duration, stalled effort, visible frustration, a familiar failed pattern, a concrete life context
- Avoid generic category language (describing everyone = describing no one)
- Let the viewer feel seen first, then introduce the product

**When to use:**
- The audience already lives with the pain
- Self-identification pulls harder than novelty
- The problem needs more specificity, not more hype

**Common failure modes:**
- Generic category language that fits everyone and therefore no one
- Pain point too general to trigger "that's me"

### Reference Examples

**Price / cost pain:**
- Why is it challenging and costly to find a good [product category]?
- Searching for an affordable [product type]? Check this out!
- Don't waste your money on [X]; instead buy [X].
- Searching for an affordable [product type]?
- Don't buy X that doesn't work. Try this instead.
- I can't believe this costs only [price].

**Failure / frustration:**
- Why you're failing at [activity]
- The worst mistake you can make in [subject]
- Avoid these common pitfalls in [topic]
- Why [common practice] is ruining your [outcome]
- What no one tells you about [issue]
- Stop doing this if you want to succeed in [field]
- The real reason you're not seeing results in [field]
- Why [habit] is wasting your time
- The biggest regret you'll have in [situation]
- The dark side of [popular trend]
- Don't be fooled by [misconception]
- The ugly truth about [common belief]
- The hidden dangers of [activity]
- How [action] is destroying your [goal]
- The shocking reality of [popular topic]
- Why [trend] is a bad idea
- The downside of [seemingly positive aspect]
- What you should never do in [activity]
- Why [common advice] is actually harmful
- Exposing the myths about [topic]

**Struggle / difficulty:**
- Struggling to do [task]?
- I've been struggling with [task], but [product] has really helped.
- If you're struggling with [X], a [X] might be the way to go.
- Dealing with [negative experience]? I used [product] to help.
- I wanted to stop doing X, so I tried this instead.
- If you have [pain point] — you need to see this!
- Something that's always annoyed me about X.
- There's nothing more painful than [X].
- Today was the worst day ever...
- I messed up...
- The biggest mistake I made was when I...
- If I had known this sooner...
- Nobody tells you how hard [X] is until you're already doing it.
- Why adults avoid [task]… [product] makes it easy.

**Emotional venting:**
- Hate to break it to you, but...
- Frankly, I'm tired of...
- It irks me so much that...
- Does anyone else hate [X]?
- Am I the only one who...
- Is there anything worse than [X]?

**Dissatisfaction with alternatives:**
- [Worse alternative] can be difficult to deal with.
- I kept experiencing [pain point], so I tried this instead!
- Something that's always annoyed me about X.
- Instead of [worse alternative], try this.
- Still using [the worse alternative to the product]? Watch this.

## 5. patternInterrupt

**Skill mapping:** `hook-design` → `patternInterrupt` mechanism

### Overview

A pattern interrupt hook uses unexpected visuals, sounds, or movements to break the scroll habit. It hijacks attention before the brain consciously decides whether to watch.

**Requirements:**
- The interrupt must connect to the content that follows — random chaos might stop the scroll, but viewers leave when they realize the video isn't what they expected
- Mute test: watch the first 3 seconds on mute and check whether the visual alone communicates the topic or value
- If the interrupt is purely decorative, it cannot sustain engagement beyond the initial hold

**Common effective forms:**
- Sudden camera movements
- Unexpected props
- Abrupt sound effects
- Visual contrast that stands out in the feed
- Starting on a close-up of an unusual object, then zooming out to reveal context (creates a micro-mystery)

**When to use:**
- Feed sameness is the problem — the first second needs to feel different immediately
- Content doesn't fit neatly into a verbal hook structure
- A jolt is needed before explanation

**Common failure modes:**
- Shock valued prioritized over coherence
- Decorative interrupt with no connection to content
- Fails mute test — no idea what the video is about without sound

### Reference Examples

**Unboxing / experience:**
- [Product] unboxing.
- What I ordered vs. what I received.
- Let's create X with [product].
- POV: You tried [product].
- Trying home remedies for X.
- My X went from this… to this… with [product].
- Before/After.
- My friend said X, so I tried [product].

**Life slice:**
- A day in the life of X.
- Get ready with me to do [task].
- "Put a finger down" [product category] edition.
- [Product category] ASMR.
- Day in the life of [X].
- BTS [Behind-the-scenes] of my [creative process].
- Come with me to...

**Engagement bait:**
- Guys, it's here…
- Let me put you on.
- Hear me out...
- Okay, imagine this...
- Watch till the end…
- This is how to have the most [insert adverb] [action] ever.

**Trend / hype:**
- TikTok made me try [product].
- Things TikTok made me try #13.
- This [product type] is going viral on [social media platform].
- This is your sign to [X].
- I literally cannot go a day without [X].
- This [X] is a game changer.
- Something that feels illegal to know is [X].
- Contrarian opinion on [X].
- Have you heard...
- Let's discuss the latest trend in [industry or profession].

**Narrative openers:**
- If I died tomorrow, this is what I would want you to know about [X].
- I have a confession about [X].
- If you're ever [X], then keep watching/reading...
- There's a really comforting fact that...
- You're going to miss out on [X] if you don't...

## 6. socialOrIdentityStake

**Skill mapping:** `hook-design` → `socialOrIdentityStake` mechanism

### Overview

A social or identity stake hook targets social drivers: tribe membership, identity, social status, confidence, dating appeal, social presentation. The pain is no longer functional ("this thing doesn't work well") but social ("this thing makes me look/feel a certain way").

**Requirements:**
- Trigger self-recognition at the identity/tribe level
- Make the stakes concrete — vague social anxiety reads as melodrama
- The product solution should be the conclusion of the social scene, not the premise

**When to use:**
- The real pain is about confidence, social image, or belonging
- Social consequences are more legible than the technical problem
- Alternative comparison, tribe switching

**Common failure modes:**
- Melodrama that overwhelms the product logic
- Social anxiety too generic to trigger self-recognition
- Brand enters too early, breaking the "this is about me" feeling

### Reference Examples

**Direct tribe targeting:**
- Hey, [customer type], you've got to try this.
- People looking for [product category], stop scrolling.
- Watch this if you X.
- If you're a fan of [X], then you should check out [X].
- If you love [X] and don't have [X], then [X].
- IDK who needs to hear this, but [X].
- If you're [X], you should probably check out [Y].
- This post/video is for [target audience with personal experience].
- You've been looking for [X].
- Still trying to figure out [X]? Here's an epic hack...

**Alternative / tribe switching:**

### Appendix B: Hook Design Principles

**Product reveal timing:**
- Do not introduce the product before the hook has created enough curiosity, proof, or self-recognition to earn it
- Default: keep the product out of the opening; if frame one reads as "this is an ad," the reveal is probably too early
- Exception: with `proofFirst`, the product may appear early, but only as supporting evidence, not as the hero object

**3-second retention:**
- The first 3 seconds are the algorithm's first checkpoint
- Videos with 65%+ retention at 3 seconds receive 4-7x more impressions than those that lose viewers immediately
- If 35%+ of viewers leave in the first 3 seconds, the hook is not strong enough
- If retention is strong for 5 seconds then drops sharply, the hook worked but the content did not deliver on the promise

**Three psychological triggers:**
- Pattern interruption: the brain's response to anything that breaks the expected flow
- Curiosity gap: tension created by an unresolved question
- Social proof: attention triggered by evidence that others found something valuable
- The strongest hooks combine at least two triggers in the first 3 seconds

**Mute test:**
- Watch the first 3 seconds on mute — does the visual alone communicate the topic or value?
- If the hook relies on captions, voiceover, or text to make sense, the visual is probably too weak

### Appendix C: Niche Examples

- **Business/Productivity:** "If you're a marketing specialist, it's your lucky day. I'm going to show you how to organically increase your brand awareness."
- **Lifestyle/Personal Development:** "Let me show you how to meditate for 5 minutes a day, over a month - you'll drastically notice a difference in your stress levels."
- **Health/Fitness:** "Here's how you can steal Steve Jobs' presentation technique and use it for yourself!"
- **Technology/Innovation:** "Trust me when I tell you... this new website, which is completely free, will revolutionize the way you work."

### Appendix D: 11 Tips for Crafting Hooks

1. **Be Direct and Specific** — Avoid vague statements. Be clear about what your video will deliver: "Want to double your income as a freelancer? Here's how you do it."
2. **Create Urgency** — Encourage viewers to act immediately: "So you're considering whether to go on a plant-based diet, let me tell you why you SHOULD."
3. **Use Relatable Scenarios** — Address common experiences or frustrations: "Here's how you stop avoiding the tasks you need to do in your work life."
4. **Identify Pain Points** — Understand what the audience struggles with: "Here's why your diet isn't working."
5. **Share Secrets and Insider Tips**
6. **Use Numbers and Lists** — Numbers create structure: "The 5 biggest changes in the tech industry you need to know."
7. **Comment Replies** — Responding to comments as a hook creates personal touch and encourages interaction.
8. **Highlight Promotions** — Feature special offers at the beginning.
9. **Unboxing Experiences** — Unboxing videos create anticipation and curiosity.
10. **Authentic Reviews** — Genuine testimonials influence viewer opinions.
11. **Use Negative Hooks** — Challenging opinions or addressing sensitive topics elicits strong reactions.

### Appendix E: FAQ

- **How long should a hook be?** The core message should land within 2-3 seconds. The stopping element (bold claim, question, visual interrupt) must happen in the first 2 seconds.
- **Can I use the same formula for every video?** Overusing one structure creates viewer fatigue. Rotate between 3-4 formulas.
- **What if my niche doesn't fit these formulas?** Focus on the underlying psychological triggers (pattern interruption, curiosity, social proof) and adapt to your content style.
- **How do I know if a hook is working?** Check the audience retention graph. >35% drop in the first 3 seconds = hook needs work.
- **Should I use text overlays?** Yes when the verbal hook is complex or needs emphasis. Keep text large, high-contrast, and uncluttered.
- **How often should I test new hooks?** Every 5-10 videos, or when a formula that used to work stops driving holds.
- **Can I combine multiple hook formulas?** Yes. The best hooks sometimes combine elements from multiple formulas. Keep it to 2 or fewer.

### Appendix F: Hook Development Process

**Step 1: Identify core value proposition**

Before writing the hook, clarify what the viewer gains. Summarize in one sentence, then test whether it can be communicated in 3 seconds through visual, verbal, or both.

**Step 2: Match formula to content type**

Educational → bold statement or question hooks. Entertainment → pattern interrupt or proof-first.

**Step 3: Write 3-5 variations**

Draft multiple versions using different formulas. Choose the one you'd most likely stop for in your own feed.

**Step 4: Visual/text mute test**

Watch the first 3 seconds on mute. Does the visual alone communicate value or create curiosity?

**Step 5: Analyze retention data and iterate**

Check the audience retention graph. First 3 seconds drop → hook problem. Strong first 5 seconds then drop → body doesn't deliver on the hook's promise.

### Appendix G: Hook Brief Examples (Diagnosis)

Use these examples to check whether `hook-design` is producing a stable handoff object. These are diagnosis examples, not copy templates.

**Review standard:** the dry run is successful when:
- the `Hook Brief` shape stays consistent
- the mechanism is explicit instead of implied
- the next skill is obvious from the output
- downstream work does not need to re-guess the opening logic

#### Example 1: Generic Pain To `painRecognition`

**Input:**

```text
I want a stronger opening for a weight-loss product.
Current line is: trying to lose weight?
```

**Output:**

```text
Hook goal: make stalled effort feel immediately personal
Audience state: frustrated after repeated failed attempts
Specific pain or desire: months of effort with no visible scale movement
Viewer self-identification cue: "I've been doing this for months and nothing changed"
Hook mechanism: painRecognition
Opening line or opening move: you've been trying to lose weight for 8 months and the scale hasn't moved
Listener reaction: wait, yes, that is exactly it
Problem deepening: the viewer has already spent effort and wants proof that this is not another reset
Product reveal rule: do not reveal the product in the opening line; reveal only after the stalled-effort problem is clearly established
Why this mechanism fits: the audience does not need more category awareness; they need immediate self-recognition
Success metric: the opening should make the viewer feel specifically seen before the product appears
Hook risks: too broad, too salesy, no self-recognition, product enters too early
Next handoff: visual-hook
```

#### Example 2: Early Product Reveal To `socialOrIdentityStake`

**Input:**

```text
Frame one shows the bottle and the speaker says this changed my hair.
```

**Output:**

```text
Hook goal: create problem recognition before product recognition
Audience state: worried their hair still looks flat or oily even after styling
Specific pain or desire: visible hair issue that affects confidence before going out
Viewer self-identification cue: "my hair still looks off even when I already got ready"
Hook mechanism: socialOrIdentityStake
Opening line or opening move: haven't you noticed your hair can ruin the whole look even when the outfit is good
Listener reaction: exactly, that is the part nobody says out loud
Problem deepening: the mismatch between a polished look and one obvious flaw makes the problem socially legible
Product reveal rule: keep the bottle out of frame one and out of the opening line
Why this mechanism fits: the pain matters because of presentation and confidence, not because hair is an abstract category problem
Success metric: frame one should show the consequence clearly enough that the viewer feels the social cost before brand recognition
Hook risks: obvious ad detection, beauty-commercial polish, no lived-in problem cue
Next handoff: visual-hook
```

#### Example 3: Benchmark-Led Leisure Opening To `questionGap`

**Input:**

```text
We have a benchmark of an attractive couple on paddleboards.
We want that kind of opening energy for our leisure kit video.
```

**Output:**

```text
Hook goal: sell the desirable day before the item list begins
Audience state: browsing for aspirational leisure content, not actively shopping yet
Specific pain or desire: wanting an easy, social, outdoors day that feels relaxed and desirable
Viewer self-identification cue: "I want that exact kind of day"
Hook mechanism: questionGap
Opening line or opening move: start on a lived-in overhead leisure shot that makes the viewer ask where they are and what is on the board
Listener reaction: wait, what are they using out there
Problem deepening: the viewer wants the scene first and only then becomes curious about the setup details
Product reveal rule: do not lead with the kit inventory; let the scene earn the curiosity first
Why this mechanism fits: the audience is not shopping with high intent yet, so desire must create the question before the product can answer it
Success metric: the first beat should generate a concrete "what is in that setup" question without reading like a kit ad
Hook risks: scenery-only opening, product list too early, couple portrait drift, polished travel-ad feel
Next handoff: reference-decode
```
